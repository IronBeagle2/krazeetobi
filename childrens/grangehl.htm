GUIDANCE NOTES FOR YOUNG ACTORS



  • Get good training - join a school drama club if you have one, or find out if there is a club or amateur society in your area.

  • Always keep in mind that it is extremely hard to make a living as an actor - for every actor who is working, thousands are not. And although a few famous people earn good money, the majority are not well paid. So you should not neglect your school work. Make sure you have plenty of other skills with which you can make a living.

  • It is extremely difficult to get a part on CBBC. Most of our directors see on average 200 children for one part. They (or their casting directors) tend to approach both stage schools and agencies, as well as ordinary schools and clubs where it is known that there is good drama teaching. But there is no list of such places. Every director has his/her own network of contacts built up from experience.

  • Most directors have contacts in different parts of the country, depending on the requirements of the script. There is a book called "Spotlight" which has photos of actors and lists their agents. There is also a "Children’s Spotlight". If you can find a copy at your local library, you might find it helpful to look at it.

  • Many young people write to us enclosing photos and CV and asking for auditions. Sadly, we simply do not have the time to see everyone, nor can we afford to pay travel expenses. Very occasionally, if someone has an exceptional CV a director might see them, but it is extremely rare.

  • If, with the agreement and support of your parents, you decide to approach an agent, the procedure is normally as follows:

    i) A meeting (accompanied by a parent) with agent to see if the agent wishes to take you onto their books.

    ii) If the agent agrees to take you on, they may ask you or your parent to sign a contract. You MUST have this checked by a lawyer - your local Citizens Advice Bureau will probably help.

    iii) The normal procedure is for agents to take a percentage (usually 10%) of money earned by a child as their fee. It is not customary for money to be requested up front prior to work, except perhaps for photographs.

    iv) We cannot supply lists of agents, but there is a book called "Contacts" which lists them and is published by:

  • If you should ever be offered a professional part on TV or in the theatre you will need the permission of your school to be absent.

    So it is important that you work hard so that your teachers would not feel your work would suffer by being away from school. (You would have to have lessons whilst filming of course).

  • Remember there are many other jobs in showbusiness apart from acting. Learning about stage management, stage design, costume and make up, sound recording, scriptwriting, choreography etc. can also be valuable. Whatever area interests you, the more experience you can get, even in an amateur capacity, the better.

    Most people who eventually make it into showbusiness have usually been doing something connected with TV or theatre from a young age, although they are very likely to have gone to acting or music college or university in between.
Good Luck!
