Thursday, March 27 at 4.35pm

The Really Wild Show Team is on Safari in Kenya in the final programme of the series.

Howie Watkins decides to do a spot of baby-sitting, but realises that it's a jumbo task when Emily, Zoe and Aitong turn out to be elephants. The keepers at Nairobi Elephant Orphanage devote their lives to caring for the baby elephants who arrive distressed at the centre after poachers have killed their parents. As elephants are normally nursed by their mothers over several years, these young orphans need constant love and affection. Howie helps bottle-feed the elephants and discovers that the keepers even sleep with them at night.

Michaela Strachan meets 15-year-old Andy Kioko, whose father is in charge of the wildlife parks in Kenya. Andy is concerned about the effects of tourism on the cheetahs at the Massai Mara Reserve which has over 250,000 visitors a year. "Over the last three years the cheetah population in the Mara has dropped by about 30%" explains Andy. The cheetahs often starve or suffer from stress-related diseases because they are constantly surrounded by tourist vehicles which get in the way of their hunting.

During their tour of the reserve, Andy and Michaela see five jeeps full of French and Italian tourists hurtle on to the scene and drive between a cheetah and her prey. The cheetah tries to carry on for a while, but finally gives up and slumps under a tree.

Michaela's next stop is Lake Naivasha in Kenya's ancient Rift Valley. The lake is home to a spectacular array of wildlife. "The Lake is one of my favourite places in the world and I couldn't wait to get back there" she says.

There, Michaela joins conservationist Damien Cook who takes her on a safari around the Lake. They encounter a group of noisy hippos, but Damien looks very nervous when several start to dive underwater. Hippos are regarded as the most dangerous animals in Africa and have been known to kill hundreds of people a year. As they creep closer to the hippos, Damien warns: "You should never get between a hippo and the water. It feels safe in water and you're in trouble if you block its escape route. They can run surprisingly fast and have such a huge mouth and strong tusks that they've even been known to chomp a lion in two!"
